# RemarkaRestore Pathway — Helping Owners Afford Major Repairs (Mill & Pave)

Prepared July 2026. Companion to `PRESENTATION_SUBSCRIPTION_PLAN.md`. That plan's
RemarkaScore rubric deliberately turns **F-grade lots away** from the flat-fee subscription
and gates **D-grade lots** behind a restoration quote. This document is the other half:
**what to sell those owners instead** — a structured way to afford mill & overlay, full-depth
repair, and repaving, so a failed lot becomes a two-stage customer (restoration project →
subscription) instead of a dead lead.

The problem it solves: a 30,000 sq ft mill & overlay is a **$45k–$90k** check. Most local
owners and small property managers don't have that sitting in this year's budget — so they
defer, the lot keeps failing, and the eventual bill doubles. The Pathway gives them three
ways to say yes anyway.

---

## 1. Cost anchors for the big line items (2026)

| Repair | National range | 30,000 sq ft example | Adds |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mill & overlay** (grind 1.5–2", fresh lift; base still sound) | **$1.50–$4.00 / sq ft** (national mid-band $1–$3) | $45k–$120k | **8–15 years** of life |
| **Full-depth reconstruction** (base failed) | roughly 2–3× mill & overlay | $100k+ | 20+ years |
| **Partial-depth patching / large-area repair** (isolated base failures) | $2–$5/sq ft hot-patch; $4–$12 saw-cut | scoped per area | bridges to a future overlay |

Two triage rules that drive every Pathway conversation:

1. **Mill & overlay only works over a sound base.** If cores/inspection show subgrade
   failure, an overlay is money buried — it reflects the cracks through in 2–3 years. The
   RemarkaScore *Pavement structure* factor (×6 weight) is the first-pass triage; anything
   ambiguous gets core sampling before a number goes on paper.
2. **Whole-lot reconstruction is rarely necessary.** Most "failed" lots are 20–40% failed.
   Saw-cut and rebuild the failed zones, mill & overlay the rest — often 40–60% cheaper than
   the repave quote the owner is dreading. That reframe alone wins bids.

## 2. The three Pathway tools

### Tool 1 — Phased restoration (zone-by-zone, worst-first)

Split the lot into zones using the RemarkaScore walk (the per-factor scores already identify
which areas are worst). Then schedule the work across **2–3 construction seasons**:

- **Season 1:** failed zones — full-depth repair where the base is gone, drainage fixes.
- **Season 2:** mill & overlay the remaining structural zones.
- **Season 3 (optional):** sealcoat + full re-stripe, then roll straight into the subscription.

Why owners take it:
- Each phase is a **separate fiscal-year budget line** — property managers can approve
  $30k/year for three years far more easily than $90k once. This matches how commercial
  budget cycles actually work.
- **The lot never fully closes.** Phasing by zone keeps tenants parking and businesses open.
- Each phase is billed on completion — **no financing, no debt, no escrow.** This is the
  default recommendation.

Caveats to engineer in: phase joints must be saw-cut clean and planned so water doesn't sit
against an unfinished edge over winter; do the drainage/base work first, cosmetics last.

### Tool 2 — Third-party financing (Todd is paid at completion)

For owners who want it done in one mobilization, partner with a commercial financing source
and pass the paper through — **never carry the loan on Remark A Pave's balance sheet.**

- Contractor-offered payment plans through finance partners are now standard in paving
  (established firms advertise "flexible payment plans" as a sales feature).
- Terms in the market run roughly **1–7 years**; owners with property income treat a
  $90k job as ~$1,400–$2,000/month instead of a capital event.
- Todd's position: get paid in full at completion by the lender; the owner repays the
  lender. Vet 2–3 local/regional options (equipment-finance shops that do project paper,
  the owner's own bank, SBA 504/7(a) for owner-occupied properties) and keep a one-page
  "financing available" flyer in the sales binder.
- **Rule: Todd never becomes the lender.** In-house installment plans mean carrying
  collection risk, cash-flow gaps, and possibly lending-law compliance. Refer the paper out.

### Tool 3 — Member pricing (the flywheel)

*(Restructured July 2026: the original "Save-to-Pave" accrued-credit idea is superseded by
the subscription's member-discount ladder — same flywheel, simpler mechanics, no
held-funds/escrow questions at all.)*

The hook that ties the two product lines together is the Presentation Plan's tiered discount
on capital work (see `PRESENTATION_SUBSCRIPTION_PLAN.md` §3): **10/15/20% off self-performed
pavement work by tier, and 5–10% off GC'd mill & pave after 12 months of active membership.**

- A D-grade owner who can't afford restoration *yet* still signs the subscription today —
  it stops the bleeding (crack fill at member pricing, cold-patch cap, documentation) and
  starts the mill & pave tenure clock. When the RemarkaReport™ photo log shows the lot's
  decline curve year over year, the restoration sells itself, at pricing the owner has
  already "earned."
- The 12-month tenure gate on mill & pave protects the thin GC markup (15–25%) from
  subscribe-today-discount-tomorrow gaming, and by the time it vests the member has paid
  a year of fees — the discount is funded.

## 3. Delivery model — GC it, don't buy iron

Remark A Pave doesn't own a milling machine or paver, and shouldn't buy one for this
(equipment financing for a paver train runs 36–84 months at 5–22% — a fleet bet, not a
product feature). The Pathway is delivered by **subbing the mill/pave work and GC'ing the
project**:

| Model | Todd's take | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **GC / prime** (recommended) | **15–25% markup** on the paving sub, plus self-performed scope | Owns the client, controls quality, keeps crack fill / seal / stripe / final detail in-house (highest-margin phases), feeds the subscription | Carries contract risk; needs a reliable paving sub + certificate of insurance flow-down |
| Referral partner | 5–10% referral fee | Zero risk | Hands the relationship to a competitor who also sells maintenance |

Self-performed scope inside every Pathway project — crack routing/fill, sealcoat, striping,
sweeping/porter detail during construction — is exactly the equipment Todd already owns.
The sub does the iron work; Remark A Pave does everything before and after it and keeps the
customer forever.

**Before launch:** line up 1–2 paving subs in the Ponca City / Kay County / Tulsa corridor,
agree markup-friendly wholesale pricing, and verify their insurance certs name Remark A Pave
as additional insured on GC'd jobs.

## 5. The sales wedge — the 5-Year Pavement Capital Plan

The deliverable that opens these doors is **free**: a one-page, 5-year capital plan generated
from the RemarkaScore walk —

- Current grade + photo baseline (RemarkaReport™ format)
- Year-by-year: what to do, when, and budget band (phases, seal cycles, restripe)
- The decline curve: what the same work costs if deferred 3 years (deferral roughly doubles
  structural repair cost — the industry's core preventive-maintenance argument)

Property managers are *required* to budget CapEx years ahead; almost nobody hands them a
pavement plan formatted for that. It costs Todd one lot walk (already happening for the
RemarkaScore) and positions Remark A Pave as the advisor, not the vendor. Offer it on the
website as the top-of-funnel offer for large properties.

## 6. Guardrails

1. **Never self-finance** (Tool 2 rule). Refer the paper; get paid at completion.
2. **Member benefits are discounts, never held funds** (Tool 3 rule). No client money is ever collected ahead of capital work; discounts are sized to per-service margin and the mill & pave % is tenure-gated.
3. **Core-sample ambiguous bases before quoting mill & overlay.** An overlay over a failed
   base is a warranty claim with a 2-year fuse.
4. **Phase joints and drainage are engineering decisions,** not sales decisions — plan the
   phase map with the paving sub before the owner sees it.
5. **Flow-down insurance on GC'd jobs** — sub's CGL naming Remark A Pave additional insured,
   plus workers' comp certs, every project.
6. **Scope creep clause:** unknown subsurface conditions (unstable subgrade, buried
   utilities, unexpected depth) are a documented change order, same discipline as the
   wide-crack exclusion in the crack-fill plan.
